## What is an Edge?

Casinos do not gamble; they execute a mathematical edge. The house knows that in roulette, if you bet on Red, you have slightly less than a 50% chance of winning because of the green zero. Over a timeline of 10,000 spins, the casino will mathematically, undeniably drain the player's account.

You must become the casino. 

A trading edge is **a set of conditions that, when aligned, provide a higher probability of one outcome occurring over another.** It does not mean you will win every trade. It means that over a series of 100 executions, the math dictates you will end up in profit.

## Eliminating Subjectivity

The number one reason retail traders fail is subjectivity. They look at a chart and "feel" like it's going to go up. 

To trade at an institutional level, you must eliminate your feelings entirely. Your trading plan must be purely mechanical. If a robot cannot be programmed to execute your rules, your rules are too vague.

## Constructing the Mechanical Plan

A professional trading plan requires strict parameters across four categories.

### 1. The Time Filter (When do I look at the chart?)
You must establish exactly what times you are allowed to execute a trade. 
*   **Bad Rule:** "I will trade whenever I have free time."
*   **Good Rule:** "I am only allowed to execute a trade between 8:00 AM EST and 11:00 AM EST (New York Killzone)."

### 2. The Narrative Filter (What am I looking for?)
Before you drop to a lower timeframe, you must have a higher timeframe (HTF) objective. This dictates your directional bias.
*   **Bad Rule:** "I will buy if the 1-minute chart looks bullish."
*   **Good Rule:** "I will only buy if the Daily chart has tapped a massive Bullish FVG and is currently aiming for a 4-hour Liquidity Pool above."

### 3. The Execution Model (What exactly triggers my entry?)
This is the core of your edge. It is the specific pattern that must form *inside* your time filter and *aligned* with your narrative filter.
*   **Bad Rule:** "I will enter when price drops a lot."
*   **Good Rule:** "Inside the NY Killzone, if price sweeps an old 15m Low, immediately reverses to break structure, and leaves a 5m Fair Value Gap, I will place a Buy Limit at the 50% mark of the FVG."

### 4. The Risk Model (How do I protect the account?)
You must clearly define where your stop loss goes and exactly what percentage of your account you are risking.
*   **Bad Rule:** "I'll risk whatever feels safe, and close it if it drops too low."
*   **Good Rule:** "I will risk exactly 1% of my account balance per trade. My Stop Loss must be placed below the candle that formed the liquidity sweep. If the Stop Loss is hit, I do not re-enter the same setup."

## Trusting the Edge (The Sample Size)

Once you define your mechanical edge, you must execute it flawlessly for a minimum of **20 trades**. 

Most traders execute a plan flawlessly for two trades. Both happen to be losers (which is normal in probability). The trader panics, assumes the edge is broken, searches YouTube for a new strategy, and starts the cycle over again. This is called **Strategy Hopping**.

If you flip a coin 4 times, it might land on heads 4 times in a row. But if you flip it 1,000 times, it will fall exactly at 50/50. Probabilities need time to play out.

Execute your exact rules 20 times, regardless of the outcome. Only then can you evaluate the data and determine if the edge is profitable.

## Creating Your Document

Do not keep your plan in your head. 

Open a blank document. Write down your Time Filter, your Narrative Filter, your Execution Model, and your Risk Model. Print it out. Put it next to your monitor.

If you are about to click `Buy` or `Sell`, look at the paper. If the current chart does not match every single rule on that paper, taking the trade is a breach of your discipline. You are no longer trading; you are gambling.
